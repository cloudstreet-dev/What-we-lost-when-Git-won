# Foreword

A book about version control written in 2026 has to start by acknowledging an awkward fact: most readers will arrive thinking the topic is settled. Git won. There are a few holdouts in games, in chip design, in regulated industries, and in research labs full of obstinate people, but the rest of software development has standardized on a tool whose model is widely treated as obvious.

It is not obvious. Almost nothing about Git is obvious. The fact that it feels that way is the artifact of fifteen years of training, tooling, and platform integration so thorough that the underlying choices have become invisible.

This book is not an attempt to talk anyone out of Git. Git is a fine system, used in places it suits and in places it doesn't, and the cost of switching is enormous even when the switch would be the right call. The point of the book is something else. The point is that *version control is a fifty-year conversation about how humans coordinate changes to shared artifacts*, and the conversation produced more than one good answer. Each system encoded a theory of how teams work — centralized authority, optimistic locking, distributed consensus, content-addressed history, patch algebra — and most of those theories have not been refuted. They have just been forgotten.

If your only mental model is Git's, you carry an unspoken set of assumptions. That history is a graph. That commits are snapshots. That branches are pointers. That the unit of review is the diff. That the canonical artifact lives on a hosted forge. None of these are wrong, but none of them are the only way. Treating them as the only way makes whole classes of problem hard that were never inherently hard, and makes whole classes of solution invisible.

The structure of the book is straightforward. Part I lays the foundation: what version control is, what design axes systems vary along, and a single shared scenario — three contributors, a rename, a binary, a conflict, a botched commit, a tag, a release — that every later chapter renders in its own idioms. Parts II through VI walk the systems themselves, roughly chronologically, from SCCS in 1972 to Jujutsu in the present. Part VII covers the forge era: GitHub, GitLab, Gerrit, and what happens when the platform absorbs functions the version control system used to own. Part VIII is the synthesis: how to choose, what the anti-patterns are, and an inventory of ideas that the industry left on the floor.

A few notes on what this book is not. It is not a tutorial. There are excellent tutorials for every system covered here, and citations to them are in Appendix D. It is not a manifesto. The closing chapter is titled "What We Lost When Git Won," but it is an inventory, not a complaint. It is also not exhaustive. Fifty years of version control includes several hundred systems if you count earnestly, and the chapters here cover only the ones whose ideas survive in some form, in some toolchain, somewhere — including ideas surviving as scars.

A note on voice. The book is opinionated. When a system is poorly designed, the book says so. When a story has been mythologized in both directions — and the BitKeeper story has been mythologized in *both* directions, which is rare and worth observing — the book attempts to tell it accurately, with sources, without taking sides. The reader should expect dryness, density, and a certain fondness for the people who tried strange and difficult things and did not quite get the world they wanted.

A note on scope. The book treats software version control as the central case but borrows freely from adjacent practices: Wikipedia's revision histories, Google Docs' operational transform, etcd and CRDTs as distributed-state cousins, and the document control systems used in regulated manufacturing. These are not version control in the strict sense, but they share a lineage and they make some of Git's choices look stranger than they otherwise would.

Read the book in order if you want; skip around if you want. The exemplar repository in Chapter 3 is the only piece you genuinely need before reading the format chapters, because every later chapter assumes you know what is being rendered. Everything else is independent enough that opening to the Mercurial chapter and reading just that one is a perfectly defensible way to use the book.

One last thing. The book is licensed CC0. There is no royalty, no syndication deal, no content-marketing pipeline behind it. It exists because someone has to write down what we know about this design space before everyone who remembers SCCS retires, and because every team that picks a version control system in 2026 deserves to know they are picking from a richer menu than the marketing suggests.

Begin with what version control actually is. The rest follows.
